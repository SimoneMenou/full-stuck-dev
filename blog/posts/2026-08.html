AUG 2026 — things I hate

10/2 things I hate about becoming a dev

Career-changer's honest take. The hard parts of switching into code, told without polishing.

  1. The imposter syndrome that erases your own code. You think of a solution. You don't dare keep it. You delete code that actually worked. You question your own logic more than what your colleagues wrote next to you. The doubt is asymmetric, and that's the worst part.
  2. The code-as-mirror moments. You open Codewars. Someone solved the kata in three lines using ASCII art and a clever LINQ trick. You look at your five-line solution that doesn't even pass all the tests. Daily brutal reality check.
  3. The "what I did today" gap. Try telling your family you spent eight hours stuck on a single line of code. Show a friend your editor for the first time. Mine didn't even want to read it. The job lives in a language people you love don't speak. Conversations stay polite. Underneath, a quiet solitude.
  4. The chair-and-steps shift. I count my steps now. I never did, back when my old job had me running across event terraces all day. I've never needed sport so badly. Not technically a hate, just a body-level change you don't see in any bootcamp brochure.
  5. The cliché inside the cliché. Christmas sweaters with binary jokes, Lego maximalism on every other desk, an IT Crowd aesthetic that wasn't supposed to be real. It changes you. There's real charm in becoming a character from that universe, and most days it's fun. Sometimes, you realize you've drifted into a language only fellow geeks speak.

And yet, I'm still here. My code is getting tighter. I've stopped trying to translate every line at dinner. I found my weird inside the IT weird. The imposter syndrome stays. The days I forget to feel it count as wins.
