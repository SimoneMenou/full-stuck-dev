10/2 things I learned about manual testing

Six months on the testing side. Five things the role taught me that I'm taking back to dev work.

  1. Stepping out of dev mode. When you're building a feature, you stay in your dev brain: your code, your data, your assumptions. Testing forces you to leave that. You step into multiple personas, the cautious user, the curious one, the one who clicks everything twice. Suddenly the feature looks different.
  2. Thinking the happy flow. Before you can test what breaks, you have to map what should work. Walking through the user's expected path, step by step, surfaces UX gaps you'd never have written into a ticket.
  3. You can't test what you don't understand. Manual testing pushes you to read the docs, talk to the PO, ask why this feature exists at all. Closer to the business than most dev work allows. You end up learning the product, not just the code.
  4. Benevolent, malevolent, distracted. Three users at minimum: the well-behaved one (happy path), the curious explorer (edge cases), the malicious one (security and abuse). Anticipating all three quietly redefines what "done" means.
  5. It makes you a better dev. Six months of finding bugs in other people's code and you start spotting them in your own before they ship. The edge-case mindset transfers back. You write defensive code without thinking about it.

Six months on Azure DevOps Test Plans and Excel sheets that grew faster than the codebase. The tooling is rarely sexy. The practice quietly changes how you think. You start writing tests in your head before code.
